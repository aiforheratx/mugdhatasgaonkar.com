The women's health problem

Women's health has a data problem that's really a priorities problem. Women were excluded from most clinical research for decades, so the conditions that shape daily life for millions of women — PMDD, perimenopause, the mental-health side of fertility treatment — arrive with the thinnest tooling, the vaguest guidance, and the least research behind them. PMDD is the sharpest example: it's debilitating, it's diagnosable only through prospective daily ratings across multiple cycles, and almost nothing helps you actually collect those ratings.

This is also the seam I know professionally. I spend my working life on clinical AI in a regulated environment. MindPulse is what happens when I point that at a problem I'd actually use.

The same argument, made in public: this conviction doesn't just live in an app. AI for Her ATX — the nonprofit I founded — publishes LinkedIn posts on women's health and AI: the research gap, cycle-aware care, and why privacy matters most for exactly this data. Its agent fleet drafts those posts with Claude and a human approves every one before it ships. MindPulse is the product side of the same argument the nonprofit makes in words.

How a day flows through it

  1. Track

    Daily mood and symptom ratings, medication stack, plus signals from HealthKit — Apple Watch and RingConn, Oura behind a config gate.

    You + sensors

  2. Ground

    A curated women's-health knowledge base grounds the AI through RAG — so answers come from vetted material, not model vibes.

    On-device RAG

  3. Reason

    Apple FoundationModels run locally with a persona and hard medical guardrails. It tracks and explains; it never diagnoses.

    On-device AI

  4. Predict

    A learned cycle engine adapts to your actual cycle instead of assuming a textbook 28 days — and flags the phase where symptoms cluster.

    Cycle engine

  5. Export

    Prospective ratings compile into a clinician PDF — the exact evidence format a diagnosis conversation needs.

    You + your doctor

How AI is used — and which AI

  • in_the_appThe app's own intelligence is Apple FoundationModels, on-device — deliberately not a cloud model. Mental-health and fertility data never earns a network round-trip. RAG grounding + persona + guardrails, with curated templates as fallback.
  • building_itClaude Code built the app — the SwiftUI architecture, the cycle engine, the guardrail design, and the full unit test suite that's currently green.
  • guardrailsHard medical boundaries: it tracks, explains, and prepares evidence. It does not diagnose, and it's engineered never to imply that it does.

What is used

  • APPSwiftUI + SwiftData, fully native iOS — a health companion that stutters is one you stop opening
  • AIApple FoundationModels on-device · RAG grounding · persona · guardrails · template fallback
  • SIGNALSHealthKit — Apple Watch, RingConn; Oura behind a config gate
  • COVERAGEDepression · Anxiety · IVF/fertility · Autism · ADHD · PMDD · Perimenopause
  • PRIVACYNo servers, no API keys, no network. There is no data pile to breach, subpoena, or monetise — it doesn't exist

Privacy as architecture

The privacy posture isn't a policy page, it's a build decision. On-device inference means the most sensitive category of data anyone has doesn't leave the phone to be useful. You can't leak what you never collected.
