Platform · The brain

Mbrain

Once you're running eight AI agents, the agents stop being the hard part. Knowing what they're all doing becomes the hard part. Mbrain is the answer to that.

The problem

I build a lot of agents. A nonprofit's marketing fleet. A research pipeline. A coach. A set of native apps. Individually each one works. Collectively they became a fog — I couldn't tell you which were running, which had quietly died, which token was about to expire and take a publishing pipeline down with it.

Every agent had its own conventions, its own credentials, its own idea of where it kept state. The fleet had grown past what I could hold in my head, which is exactly the point at which most side-project sprawl starts rotting.

What it is

A single source of truth for the whole fleet, plus the machinery to keep it honest.

  • A registry — one canonical record of every agent: what it is, where it lives, how it runs.
  • Live status detection — probes what's actually running right now rather than trusting what a config file claims.
  • A neural command centre — a brain-hub map of every agent and connector, with a live reporting console.
  • Connectors — Gmail, LinkedIn, and Instagram wired once, centrally, instead of re-solved per project.
  • A credential vault and expiry monitor — a daily job that re-probes every token against its provider and emails me before anything expires.
  • A self-updating profile — a weekly headless job that re-reads my own history and rewrites the brain's picture of what I'm working on.

The idea underneath

The credential monitor is the part I'd point at. It doesn't read a stored expiry date and trust it — it asks each provider directly, every morning, and tells me seven days out. The difference matters: stored state drifts, and the failure mode of a silently-expired token is a publishing pipeline that looks healthy and posts nothing.

That's the whole philosophy in miniature. Probe reality, don't trust the record. Live status detection over declared status. Verified tokens over remembered ones. It's the same instinct that governance work in healthcare teaches you: the system that says it's fine is not the same as the system that is fine.
